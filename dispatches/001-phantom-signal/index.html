Phantom Signal

DISPATCH 001 // 2026.03.15 // FIELD CORRESPONDENT: K. VASQUEZ

The relay at station 14-North picked it up first — a low, rhythmic pulse bleeding through the static on channel 7.3. Survey protocol says log it, flag it, move on. But Vasquez had been running the northern exclusion zone routes for three years, and she'd never heard anything on 7.3. Nobody had. The channel was dead spectrum, allocated to a transmitter network that was decommissioned before she was born.

She ran the signal through the array's pattern analyzer. The pulse wasn't random noise. It had structure — repeating intervals, frequency modulation that looked almost like compression artifacts. Like something had been encoded and was now decaying, the message degrading with each cycle but still stubbornly repeating itself into the void. The array's automated systems had been filtering it out as background noise for who knows how long.

Station 14-North sits at the edge of the exclusion zone, where the old industrial grid gives way to empty terrain that hasn't been surveyed in over a decade. Whatever is transmitting out there isn't on any registry. Vasquez filed the report and flagged it for technical review, but the review queue is backed up by months. In the meantime, the signal keeps pulsing. Every forty-seven seconds. Like clockwork. Like it's waiting.
